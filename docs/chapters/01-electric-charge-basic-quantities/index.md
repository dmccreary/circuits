**Key Takeaways**

1. Electric charge is the fundamental property that drives all electrical phenomena, measured in coulombs — and current is simply charge in motion, measured in amperes.
2. Voltage is the potential difference (the "push") that drives current through a circuit; resistance is the opposition to that flow; and Ohm's Law (V = IR) links the three.
3. Power (P = VI) is the rate of energy transfer, and every resistor has a maximum power rating that must not be exceeded.

## Summary

### Key Concepts

- **Electric charge** (Q) is the fundamental property of matter that creates electric forces; measured in coulombs (C)
- **Current** (I) is the rate of charge flow past a point; measured in amperes (A = C/s)
- **Voltage** (V) is electric potential difference — energy per unit charge between two points; measured in volts (V = J/C)
- **Resistance** (R) opposes current flow; measured in ohms (Ω)
- **Power** (P) is the rate of energy conversion; measured in watts (W = J/s)
- A **node** is a junction where two or more components connect; a **branch** is a single component path
- **Ground** is the reference point (0 V) — all voltages are measured relative to it

### Important Equations

\[ I = \frac{dQ}{dt} \qquad P = VI \qquad P = I^2R = \frac{V^2}{R} \]

### What You Should Understand

- Voltage is always measured *between two points*, never at a single point
- Conventional current flows from + to −; electron flow is opposite
- SI prefixes (milli-, kilo-, mega-) apply to all circuit quantities
- Ground is a reference, not a physical sink for charge

### Applications

- Battery and power supply voltage/current ratings
- Fuse and circuit breaker sizing for overcurrent protection
- Energy consumption calculations for electronic devices
